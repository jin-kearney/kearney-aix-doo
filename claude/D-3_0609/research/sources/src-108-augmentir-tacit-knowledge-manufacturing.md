## Relevant Excerpt

### Definition of Tacit Knowledge
Tacit knowledge refers to the unwritten, experience-based knowledge that individuals gain through hands-on practice, intuition, and personal insights. Unlike explicit knowledge (manuals, procedures, training materials), tacit knowledge is often difficult to articulate or transfer because it is deeply rooted in personal experiences, observations, and skills.

### Why Tacit Knowledge Has Been Hard to Capture in Manufacturing
- **Workforce Turnover & Retirement** – Skilled workers leave without passing down expertise
- **Lack of Documentation** – Knowledge is informal and not recorded in SOPs
- **Varied Learning Styles** – Hands-on workers struggle with text-heavy training
- **Inefficiencies in Knowledge Transfer** – Traditional training methods are slow and inconsistent

### AI-Powered Capture Methods (Augmentir/Augie)
1. **Real-Time AI-Powered Guidance** – AI analyzes worker performance and provides real-time adaptive work instructions
2. **Automated Knowledge Capture** – Workers use AI-driven voice, video, and text tools to document expertise in real time, creating digital work instructions
3. **Smart Digital Work Instructions** – Converts tacit knowledge into structured, AI-driven Standard Operating Procedures (SOPs) that can be continuously improved
4. **AI-Driven Workforce Insights** – AI analyzes performance data to identify skill gaps

### Key Insight for Prompt Asset Management
"AI-powered Connected Worker (CW) solutions like Augmentir are revolutionizing the way manufacturers capture, digitize, and share tacit knowledge. Augie converts tacit knowledge into structured, AI-driven Standard Operating Procedures (SOPs) that can be continuously improved."

This parallels the prompt asset management challenge: capturing the tacit judgment criteria of domain experts (e.g., quality inspectors, maintenance engineers) and converting them into structured, versioned prompts that can be reused reliably.

### Manufacturing-Specific Knowledge at Risk
- Quality inspection judgment criteria (e.g., "what defects require rejection vs. rework")
- Troubleshooting heuristics ("when a machine vibrates at this frequency, check X first")
- Process adjustment logic ("if temperature rises above Y during this phase, reduce feed rate by Z")
